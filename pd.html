Non-Black Allyship

Overview
Using persuasive design methods, our team worked to create an intervention to help drive non-Black allies to take a more proactive role to fight anti-Black racism. We created an accountability app that generates different actions for allies to take based on weekly goals. By completing tasks and meeting their monthly goals, allies can raise money to donate to the anti-racism organization of their choice.

Project Details
Context: Persuasive Design Course, CMU
Team: Kraig Fujii, Ally Hopping, Janice Lyu, Divya Mohan, Jenny Xin
Role: Bias Monitor
Timeline: August-December, 2020

Problem
While most of our participants recognized racial bias as a problem, many were unsure or unsure of what actions to take in addressing racism as an ally. Some had taken action in their own communities, but nearly all participants admitted to a lack of diversity in their own social circles, and a desire to improve.

Solution
An accountability app that allies use to set monthly goals to complete anti-racist actions that involve educating themselves, engaging members of their social circles in discussions about racism, and involvement with community anti-racist organizations.

My Role
I served as the Bias Monitor in the group, a role specifically designated to evaluate and call out how bias might affect our design process and final intervention. I recruited and conducted interviews with racial justice allies, and worked with the team to synthesize our research into concepts and designs based on various persuasive methods.

Process

New Metaphor Exercise
Originally, our team had decided to specifically target non-Black college students for our intervention. I facilitated a focus group with four graduate students from colleges in Pittsburgh in which the participants selected metaphors that reflected their perspectives on various concepts related to racial bias and discrimination on college campuses. Through this exercise, we found that students are uncertain about what specific or concrete steps to take to engage in anti-racist work. As a result, we see a lack of action and participation from students despite strong support to combat anti-Black racism. We planned to further explore the shared disconnect between attitude and behavior.

User Interviews
In continuing research on allyship, I conducted an interview with a graduate student and member of our target audience. We discussed the larger systemic racism built into settings like schools and hospitals. When it came to their own social sphere, however, my participant echoed the sentiment of other interviewees that addressing racism within their own social sphere was a challenge. They weren't sure of the exact actions to take to address that, but acknowledged that it was something they wanted to improve.

Expert Interviews
As a team, we also conducted interviews with various experts in the field of diversity and racial bias. We discussed the challenges BIPOC students face, student campus resources, pain points with these resources, and effective/ineffective allyship. One emerging theme that was discussed was the need for allies to step out of their comfort zones and make a conscious effort to engage with and expand their typical social circle.

Project Definition
After conducting the user and expert interviews, we had initially decided to create an intervention aimed at reducing racial bias on college campuses and motivating non-Black students who view themselves as allies (or recognize racial bias as an ongoing issue) to take concrete action against racism. We began to generate design ideas centered around encouraging more meaningful relationship between racially-diverse groups of students. As many of our ideas could be used in a larger context, however, we decided to expand our target audience to all motivated non-Black allies.

Ideate
From a pool of ninety ideas, we selected three potential interventions to pitch and receive feedback:

  • Our plan is for an incentivized app that breaks down allyship into manageable tasks. This app will emulate that by giving users a choice of how they’d like to be active in the Black Lives Matter movement - through donating, educating themselves, e-mailing officials, etc. It will then provide them with a task to complete or an article to read by the end of that week. Users will be further motivated by the ability to buy furniture, grow their forest, gain badges, or other artifacts as they complete more activities. In addition to the manageable tasks feature on the app, the app would have an additional “on this day” feature that notifies users of specific race-related historical incident, movement, or other POC’s effort that happened on that same day.
  • The setting of the game would be a post-apocalyptic world where the player is the main character and must work with others to survive. In one of the research papers the group read, a recommended strategy to engage allies is to encourage them to put others’ needs before their own and to reduce uncertainty in their ability to evaluate and confirm racist incidents. In the game, we would place the player in uncertain circumstances and steer them to put their needs before their team’s at the beginning of the game, which would lead to repeated failure. We would eventually steer the player to be more team-oriented, and begin to shift their priorities to helping others and becoming more secure in their evaluations of different scenarios.
  • After talking to our experts, we learned that it’s important for allies to integrate with different groups. We also learned from our users that they have a desire to diversify their social circles but do not know where to start. So our second idea is a web/app service similar to Airbnb Online Experiences where hosts share their hobbies, stories, or skills with guests. The guest chooses a category they’re interested in and the app/website pairs the guest with a host who offers an experience in that category. The goal is to create shared experiences between people of diverse backgrounds and offer a starting point for relationships to form. By pairing up diverse people together, the platform can help re-establish ingroups by drawing focus on people’s shared interests and help break down racial biases. Ultimately, by creating moments of connection, our users can find common ground and build empathy.
